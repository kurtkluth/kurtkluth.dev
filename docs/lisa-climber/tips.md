---
title: Tips
description: Practical advice for climbing higher, dying less, and keeping the score multiplier alive.
---

Eight pieces of advice from the developer, offered in roughly the order the
mountain will teach them to you anyway; this way just costs fewer hearts.

## Plan the route before you jump

Every layer is a small puzzle. Look up, pick the platforms you actually
want, and then move. Climbers who improvise mid-air tend to donate their
hearts to the kill floor.

## Treat ice like ice

Frostpeak hands you icy platforms immediately, and they are exactly as
slippery as they look. Expect the slide: start your movements earlier,
stop sooner than feels natural, and leave more landing room than you
think you need.

## Learn the icicles' timing

Icicles hang before they drop, and each one falls on a rhythm. Watch a
full cycle from somewhere safe before you cross underneath. Waiting one
beat below an icicle lane is always cheaper than a heart.

## Keep the multiplier fed

Gems and money bags raise your score and your multiplier, and the
multiplier is where big scores come from. Collect steadily (weave pickups
into your natural route instead of sprinting past them) and the same
climb is suddenly worth much more.

## Don't get greedy either

A detour for a shiny pickup is great right up until it parks you under an
icicle or in a critter's path. A heart is worth more than any single gem.
If the route to a pickup looks expensive, it is.

## Don't linger near the bottom

The red kill floor is patient, and you should not be. The lowest layers
are for leaving. Climb out of the danger zone early rather than tidying
up every last pickup down there.

## Respect the critters' rhythm

Patrolling critters are predictable, and that is their whole weakness.
Watch the patrol, find the gap, and cross behind one rather than racing
its face. They never improvise; you should not have to either.

## Let the demo teach you

The attract mode plays the game in front of you for free. Between runs,
watch it for a minute; platform handling, icicle timing, and critter
patterns all demonstrate themselves while your thumbs rest.
